Soil is a stress-dependent material: its stiffness, strength, and dilatancy all depend on the confining stress it feels. A 1:100 scale model of a monopile foundation tested on the laboratory floor therefore behaves nothing like the real thing — the stresses in the model soil are 100 times too low, and the soil is correspondingly too dilative and too stiff relative to the prototype.

The geotechnical centrifuge solves this elegantly. By spinning the model at an enhanced acceleration of *N* × g, the self-weight stresses at homologous depths in the model match those in the full-scale prototype. Stress–strain behaviour is then reproduced correctly, and the response measured in the model can be translated to prototype scale through well-defined scaling laws.

This is what makes centrifuge modelling one of the most powerful tools in geotechnical engineering: it delivers **prototype-stress data under controlled, repeatable conditions**, at a fraction of the cost and duration of field testing, and with the freedom to test to failure — something rarely possible in the field.

Typical applications include:

* Foundations and anchors for offshore wind and oil & gas (monopiles, suction caissons, jack-ups, plate anchors)
* Cyclic and long-term loading — from wave and storm sequences to thousands of load cycles
* Installation processes — pile driving, suction installation, penetrometer testing in flight
* Slopes, dams, tunnels, and earthquake response using shaking tables in flight
