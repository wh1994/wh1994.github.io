Centrifuge modelling has a longer — and stranger — pedigree than most people expect: an idea born in nineteenth-century France, realised simultaneously (and independently) in Depression-era New York and the Soviet Union, hidden behind the Iron Curtain for forty years, and dramatically revealed to the West at a Moscow conference in 1973.

## 1869 — The idea

Édouard Phillips, a French engineer of English descent working in the railway and mining industries, published the seminal paper in the *Mémoires de l'Académie des Sciences* in 1869. He recognised that elastic theory could not handle complex structures, introduced scaling relationships for models, and — for the case where self-weight matters — proposed spinning reduced-scale models in a centrifuge to restore the missing body forces. His worked examples were the Britannia Bridge over the Menai Strait and a hypothetical bridge across the English Channel. In a second paper the same year he extended the idea to dynamics, correctly concluding that inertial time scales with length — exactly as centrifuge modellers assume today. The idea was decades ahead of the technology: there is no record of anyone spinning a model in the nineteenth century.

## 1931–1936 — Realisation, twice over

The practice began almost simultaneously on two continents, apparently independently:

* **USA** — Philip B. Bucky at Columbia University, working on deep mining and strata mechanics, made the first mention of applied centrifuge modelling in 1931, using a machine of just 220 mm working radius (later 0.6 m). Terzaghi — famously sceptical of model testing, and who twice nearly moved to Columbia in the 1930s — nonetheless cited Bucky's work in *Theoretical Soil Mechanics* (1943). The technique was, however, not taken up by the American soil mechanics community for decades.
* **USSR** — In 1932, within months of each other, N. N. Davidenkov (who had been generating enhanced body forces by decelerating models dropped onto springs) and G. Y. Pokrovsky independently proposed the centrifuge. The first tests — earth embankment slope stability in sandy soils — ran in December 1932 at the Moscow Institute of Hydraulic Engineering and Hydrology, and Pokrovsky's 1933 paper in the *Journal of Technical Physics* became the founding publication of the field. At the very first ICSMFE (Harvard, 1936), Pokrovsky & Fedorov introduced the technique to the international community.

## 1936–1973 — Behind the curtain

In the USSR the technique never stopped: Fedorov designed a series of machines (the 1936 Fundamentstroi centrifuge was the most powerful in the country) and centrifuge studies supported dams, canals, mine-waste heaps, and — reflecting Pokrovsky's parallel career as a Major-General and explosives expert — military research. Almost none of it reached the West. In the USA, meanwhile, activity was limited to mining and rock mechanics (Bureau of Mines, Colorado School of Mines), and the soil mechanics community stayed away.

The turning point came at the 8th ICSMFE in **Moscow, 1973**. At a special symposium, the 72-year-old Pokrovsky revealed the scope of forty years of Soviet work and handed Andrew Schofield copies of two of his books, which were subsequently translated into English. The disclosure galvanised Western activity — including US cratering research on the Boeing centrifuge in Seattle from 1976.

## 1960s–1980s — The modern era

Independently of the Soviet programme, the modern discipline took shape in the mid-1960s, driven by **Andrew Schofield** (Cambridge, then UMIST — linking the centrifuge to critical state soil mechanics and rigorous scaling practice) and, in parallel, **Masato Mikasa** in Japan, whose consolidation and rockfill-dam studies seeded what is now the world's densest concentration of industry-operated centrifuges. **Peter Rowe** built a large machine at Manchester (2.0 × 1.0 m models at 120g) aimed squarely at design: his programmes underpinned the Oosterschelde Storm Surge Barrier and North Sea gravity platforms — perhaps the closest the centrifuge has come to being *necessary* for design. Purpose-built machines followed across Europe, Japan, and North America, with earthquake-simulation capability arriving in the 1980s.

## 1990s–present — Maturity

The technique matured into a standard research and design-verification tool: in-flight robots and actuators, digital image acquisition, miniature in-flight site investigation, drum centrifuges, and tsunami and rainfall simulators. The community is coordinated through [ISSMGE TC104](https://tc104-issmge.com/) and the four-yearly ICPMG conference series — and, as the [facilities survey](/centrifuge-101/04-facilities/) shows, the field is currently in a new construction boom centred on East Asia.
