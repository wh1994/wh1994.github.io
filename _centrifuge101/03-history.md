Centrifuge modelling has a longer pedigree than most people expect:

* **1869** — Édouard Phillips, a French engineer, first proposed using centrifugal acceleration to satisfy similitude in scaled structural models — an idea far ahead of the technology of his day.
* **1930s** — The idea was realised nearly simultaneously on two continents: Philip Bucky at Columbia University used a small centrifuge for mining-roof models, while G. Y. Pokrovsky and colleagues in the Soviet Union developed the technique systematically for geotechnical problems.
* **1960s–1980s** — The modern era began, driven above all by Andrew Schofield and the Cambridge group, who linked centrifuge testing to critical state soil mechanics and established rigorous scaling practice. Dedicated machines appeared across Europe, Japan, and North America.
* **1990s–present** — The technique matured into a standard research and design-verification tool: beam and drum centrifuges, in-flight robots and actuators, earthquake shakers, and miniature in-flight site investigation tools. Today the international community is coordinated through TC104 of the ISSMGE and the ICPMG conference series.
