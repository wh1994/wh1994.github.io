Centrifuge modelling has a longer pedigree than most people expect:

* **1869** — Édouard Phillips of the École Centrale/École Polytechnique in France published the first paper proposing centrifugal acceleration to satisfy similitude in scaled models — an idea far ahead of the technology of his day.
* **1930s–1950s** — The idea was realised nearly simultaneously on two continents: Philip Bucky at Columbia University used a small centrifuge to test the structural integrity of underground rock formations, while Soviet researchers Davidenkov and Pokrovsky developed the technique systematically — much of it for military purposes.
* **1960s** — The first purpose-built geotechnical centrifuges with simple data acquisition systems were manufactured at Cambridge University and Osaka City University.
* **1970s** — Large machines arrived, together with rapid progress in computer data acquisition, at Cambridge, LMS (France), Osaka City University, and the University of Manchester. Andrew Schofield and the Cambridge group linked centrifuge testing to critical state soil mechanics and established rigorous scaling practice.
* **1980s** — Earthquake-simulation capability (in-flight shakers) was developed at Caltech, UC Davis, Cambridge, LCPC (France), and Japanese institutions (Tokyo Institute of Technology, PWRI, PHRI).
* **1990s–present** — The technique matured into a standard research and design-verification tool: geo-environmental testing, in-flight robots and actuators, digital image acquisition, and miniature in-flight site investigation tools. Today the international community is coordinated through [TC104 of the ISSMGE](https://tc104-issmge.com/) and the ICPMG conference series.
